# Carcassonne

## Overview

Carcassonne, designed by Klaus-Jurgen Wrede and published in 2000, is a tile-laying game for 2-5 players. Players draw and place landscape tiles to build the medieval French countryside, then deploy followers (called meeples) to score points from completed features like cities, roads, monasteries, and farms. A typical game lasts 30-45 minutes. The game won the Spiel des Jahres award in 2001 and has spawned numerous expansions.

Carcassonne is celebrated for its elegant simplicity and spatial puzzle element. Each tile placement creates new opportunities and challenges, and the competition over shared features adds a layer of tactical depth. The game works well at all player counts and is easy to teach to new players.

## Setup

1. Place the start tile (marked with a darker back) face up in the center of the table. This tile shows a road, a city segment, and fields.
2. Shuffle the remaining 71 land tiles and place them in face-down stacks accessible to all players.
3. Each player takes 7 meeples (follower pieces) of their chosen color. One meeple per player is placed on the scoring track at the 0 position.
4. The youngest player goes first, with play proceeding clockwise.
5. No other board or additional setup is needed. The playing area expands as tiles are placed throughout the game.

## Turn Structure

On each turn, a player performs these steps in order:

1. **Draw and place a tile.** The player draws one random land tile and places it adjacent to an existing tile so that all edges match (roads connect to roads, cities connect to cities, fields connect to fields). A tile must be placed if there is any legal placement available. In rare cases where no legal placement exists, the tile is discarded and a new one is drawn.

2. **Optionally deploy a meeple.** The player may place one meeple from their supply onto the tile they just placed. The meeple must go on a specific feature of that tile: a city segment (as a knight), a road segment (as a highwayman), a monastery (as a monk), or a field (as a farmer, placed lying down). A meeple cannot be placed on a feature that already has any meeple on it, even if the connected feature includes segments placed by other players. However, separate features may later become connected through tile placement, resulting in shared or contested features.

3. **Score completed features.** If the tile placement completes any feature, it is scored immediately. A completed city (fully enclosed by walls) scores 2 points per tile in the city plus 2 points per pennant symbol. A completed road (both ends terminated by a crossing, city, or monastery) scores 1 point per tile in the road. A completed monastery (surrounded on all 8 sides by tiles) scores 9 points. After scoring, the meeples on the completed feature are returned to their owners' supply.

When multiple meeples occupy a contested feature due to tiles connecting separate segments, the player with the most meeples scores the full points. In a tie, all tied players receive the full points. Farmers are never returned during the game and are only scored at game end.

## Win Conditions

The game ends when the last land tile has been placed. Final scoring then occurs: incomplete cities score 1 point per tile and 1 per pennant, incomplete roads score 1 point per tile, and incomplete monasteries score 1 point plus 1 for each surrounding tile. Farmers score 3 points for each completed city their field touches. The player with the highest total score wins. In case of a tie, the tied players share the victory.

## Components

- 72 land tiles (including 1 start tile) depicting city segments, roads, monasteries, and fields
- 40 meeples (8 per player in 5 colors, including 1 scoring meeple each)
- 1 scoring track
- 1 rules booklet
- 1 tile reference sheet showing all tile types and their distribution
